# Physical Reasoning And Measurement

Define systems and quantities; measure, estimate, graph, scale, model, and check physical claims.

## Scope

- Subject: `physics`
- Learner level: foundational, with no assumed physics vocabulary
- Confirmed mathematical prerequisite: `mathematics/elementary-algebra-and-functions`
  (including its arithmetic prerequisite); no tools are assumed
- Confirmed subject prerequisites: none
- What mastery enables: choose a system and measurable quantities; express and
  convert quantity values; plan and interpret measurements; use uncertainty,
  graphs, scaling, dimensions, and estimates; construct, test, limit, and
  communicate simple physical models
- Deliberate exclusions: force, motion terminology, energy, momentum, fields,
  waves, thermal mechanisms, circuits, and other phenomenon-specific physics;
  formal statistical inference and advanced instrumentation belong to later
  decks

This deck teaches a physics-specific way of turning a question about the world
into a bounded system, measurable quantities, a purposeful model, a prediction,
and an evidence-based check. SI is the primary unit system. Models and
measurements are always interpreted for a stated purpose and under stated
conditions; neither a model nor a reported measurement is treated as an exact,
context-free copy of reality.

The VIM is a technical vocabulary, not a novice lesson sequence. This deck uses
accessible original wording while preserving its distinctions. Chapter 1 uses
simple length and count situations only; later chapters introduce fuller
metrological precision and uncertainty conventions.

## Practice outside SRS

Keep a measurement-and-model notebook. For ordinary objects, write a question,
draw the chosen system boundary, name the quantity to be measured, record the
procedure and result with units, state the model assumptions, and compare a
prediction with an observation. Later chapters should be paired with real scale
reading, repeated measurements, hand-drawn graphs, estimation, and short written
arguments; cards maintain the decisions but do not replace that practice.
